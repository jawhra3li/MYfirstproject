The Problem

Class Imbalance Breaks These Metrics

When one class far outnumbers the other, all three metrics can paint a dangerously false picture.

⚠ Classic Trap

A fraud detector on data that is 99% non-fraud achieves 99% accuracy by simply predicting "not fraud" for every transaction — yet it catches zero fraud cases.

🎯 Best operating point

The top-left corner (TPR = 1, FPR = 0) is the ideal — perfect sensitivity with no false alarms.

📐 Diagonal = random

The diagonal from (0,0) to (1,1) is a random classifier. Any useful model must curve above it.

🔁 All thresholds at once

Every point on the curve is one threshold — ROC shows the full performance envelope, not just a single cut-off.

AUC — Area Under the ROC Curve
What is AUC?

A Single Number for Overall Quality

AUC measures the area under the ROC curve — it summarises all possible thresholds into one score that reflects how well the model ranks positives above negatives.

1.0
Perfect ClassifierAll positives ranked above all negatives — ideal but rarely achieved in practice.
0.8+
Good to ExcellentStrong discriminative ability; useful for most real-world applications.
0.5
Random GuessingModel has no discriminative power — equivalent to flipping a coin.
<0.5
Worse than RandomModel is systematically wrong — flip predictions to improve it!

🔑 Why AUC beats Accuracy

AUC is threshold-independent — it evaluates the model across all possible cut-offs simultaneously. Two models with the same accuracy can have very different AUC scores, revealing which one truly generalises better.

Robustness & Selection

Insensitive to Class Skew

Because TPR and FPR are each computed within their own class, adding 10× more negatives does not shift the ROC curve or AUC — making it reliable even on heavily imbalanced data.

✅ Immune to Class Imbalance

Whether your data is 50/50 or 1/99, the ROC curve and AUC remain the same. Accuracy would swing wildly; AUC stays stable — critical for fraud detection, medical diagnosis, and anomaly detection.

Model Selection with ROC + AUC
1

Compare AUC scores across candidate models — pick the one with the highest AUC as your starting point.

2

Inspect the ROC curve shape — a high AUC but poor curve at low FPR may still fail for high-precision tasks.

3

Choose your threshold based on business cost: minimise FP (precision-critical) or maximise TP (recall-critical).
